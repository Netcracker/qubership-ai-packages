# Phase 1 Discovery — Editorial Rule Sources for an LLM Skill Writing/Editing French Software Prose

## 1. Executive summary

- **A single proven, ready-made French LLM skill for software writing does not exist.** The closest named-author SKILL.md packs (`alxbd/boileau`, `numerique-gouv/dsfr-skill`, `StealthyLabsHQ/ai-edu-skills-FR`) each cover only one slice — AI-tic removal, government design-system docs, or academic French — and none of them is a software-documentation editorial skill. Anthropic's own `anthropics/skills` repo ships no French skill, and no credible French `.cursorrules` or Continue.dev rule pack was discoverable. A French software-writing skill will have to be **assembled** from category-3 (editorial), category-2 (vendor localization), and category-4 (linter) sources.
- **Strongest category: French UI/localization style guides from major vendors and OSS projects** — Microsoft's downloadable fr-FR and fr-CA Localization Style Guides, the Mozilla L10n French style guide, and the WordPress.com French translation style guide give explicit, worked rules on tone, vous/tu, infinitive vs. impératif, anglicisms, gender, plurals, punctuation spacing, and product-name handling. These translate almost directly into SKILL.md rules.
- **Weakest / most polluted category: "best French grammar checker" listicles and prompt-marketplace pages.** They keep surfacing for any French-writing query but offer no editorial signal. Also weak: French sections of generic "AI humanizer" and SEO rewriting tools (explicitly excluded by the brief).
- **UI/localization sources are unusually strong for French**: Microsoft fr-FR + fr-CA, Mozilla fr, WordPress.com fr, KDE Francophone, GNOME-fr (traduc.org) and Debian/Ubuntu l10n-fr all publish actively-used, named-maintainer guidance.
- **Developer-documentation-specific French sources are weaker.** GitLab's documentation style guide is rich but English-only; its French side is just a Crowdin translation workflow with a glossary, not editorial rules. The DSFR site documents UI components in French but is not a prose style guide for developer docs.
- **France French and Canadian/Québec French need separate variants.** Microsoft already ships two distinct PDFs. Terminology bodies diverge meaningfully: OQLF/GDT pushes *courriel, clavardage, baladodiffusion, témoin (cookie), pourriel*, while FranceTerme accepts *mél, mail, podcast, cookie, spam* more readily; OQLF also tolerates tutoiement in more contexts where France French defaults to "vous". Microsoft's fr-FR guide is explicit on gender too: "When addressing users, always use the masculine gender. Example: You're connected to the Internet → Vous êtes connecté à Internet" (Microsoft French Style Guide §3.1.8, June 2017) — a rule that the Bureau de la traduction's écriture-inclusive guidelines and the Canada.ca content guide treat differently. Bureau de la traduction's *Clés de la rédaction* and Canada.ca content guide reflect Canadian usage.
- **Phase 2 should focus on a combination: (i) vendor localization guides for tone, anglicism and UI-string rules, (ii) FranceTerme + GDT/BDL for terminology, (iii) Imprimerie nationale *Lexique* for typography/punctuation, (iv) Grammalecte + LanguageTool-fr as the mechanical-check layer, and (v) the named-author SKILL.md packs (Boileau especially) for AI-tic removal heuristics.** Pure prose-style sources (Bon usage, Lexique IN) belong as background; the SKILL.md itself should pull operational rules from category 2 (UI) and category 4 (linter) sources.

## 3. Promising shortlist for Phase 2

1. **Microsoft fr-FR + fr-CA Localization Style Guides** — paired, they are the single best source of *concrete* rewrite rules for software French: tone, articles, possessives, semicolons banned, "le ou les périphériques" instead of "le(s) périphérique(s)", masculine for ungendered subjects, conversational conjunctions. They convert almost line-for-line into SKILL.md DO/DON'T entries and let us surface fr-FR vs fr-CA divergences in one move.
2. **Mozilla L10n French style guide + Mozilla general guide (GitHub)** — community-authored, PR-reviewed, CC-licensed; concrete on natural expression vs. literal translation, Mozilla product-name rules, and explicit per-product formality ("formal tone on Firefox Desktop and informal tone on Firefox for Android and web content"). Compresses well; lives in the same world as devs (Markdown in GitHub).
3. **WordPress.com French style guide** — short, opinionated, and software-grade: vouvoiement, *infinitif* for items that don't end in a period, *impératif* for sentences, no familier, écriture épicène, no anglicisms. Excellent as a "compact rule digest" reference for SKILL.md scaffolding.
4. **OQLF Vitrine linguistique (BDL + GDT) and FranceTerme** — paired, they form the terminology layer. Both publish IT-specific vocabularies. Phase 2 should extract a short curated glossary (cookie/témoin, email/courriel/mél, podcast/baladodiffusion, spam/pourriel, machine learning/apprentissage automatique) with locale switches.
5. **Bureau de la traduction *Clés de la rédaction* + Canada.ca Content Style Guide** — the authoritative French plain-language source for Canadian usage, with explicit anglicism and typography articles; the Canada.ca guide is unusual in giving structural plain-language techniques alongside punctuation rules. Best for editing/rewrite rules and for the Québec variant.
6. **Lexique des règles typographiques en usage à l'Imprimerie nationale** — the canonical reference for French typography. Provides the rule set for the mechanical layer (NBSP before « ; ! ? : », guillemets français with NBSP, capitales accentuées, abbreviations like "1re" not "1ère", no italic spacing tricks). Best used as a *linter* spec rather than embedded in the LLM skill.
7. **Grammalecte + LanguageTool fr module + Hunspell-fr** — the mechanical-checks trio. Grammalecte was re-activated with v2.3.0 on 15 Dec 2025 under Algoo's incoming stewardship; LanguageTool's `fr` module carries dedicated `CAT_ANGLICISMES`, `CAT_REGIONALISMES` and false-friends categories. Phase 2 should treat these as the *out-of-skill* layer: rules that belong in CI, not in the prompt.
8. **alxbd/boileau (SKILL.md)** — small but directly on-target: it codifies the exact failure modes the brief lists ("permet de", "Par ailleurs", "De plus", anglicism calques, faux registre soutenu). Even if low-evidence, it's worth examining for prompt structure and rule wording — likely the best existing template for the AI-tic-removal portion of our skill.
9. **DSFR documentation + numerique-gouv/dsfr-skill** — together they pin down sober, accessible, institutional fr-FR UI prose (button labels, formulaire text, accessibility wording). Useful when the LLM has to write or edit French UI in regulated/public contexts.
10. **ICU MessageFormat + CLDR French plural rules** — not optional. Any UI/error-message skill must encode French plural-category behaviour (0 and 1 → "one", others → "other") and ICU `{n, plural, ...}` syntax. Should become a prescriptive rule section, not just background.

## 4. Obvious rejects

- **"Top 10 best French grammar checker" listicles** (grammark.org, danalearningcenters.ca, frenchclass.in, wproofreader.com comparison blog). Affiliate/SEO content, no editorial authority, recycle the same Grammalecte/LanguageTool/Scribens/Reverso pitches. No software-writing rules.
- **Reverso, Scribens, Bon Patron, WhiteSmoke, Sapling AI, MerciApp, FrenchCorrector**. Either learner-oriented grammar checkers or closed-source paraphrasers; some skew toward AI-humanizer territory. Excluded per brief.
- **Antidote (Druide informatique)** as a primary rule source. Excellent commercial tool, but the rule set is not publicly extractable; only the Antidote blog content is reusable, and only as background.
- **Grevisse *Le bon usage*** as a SKILL.md input. Reference grammar, not a software-writing style guide; far too detailed and academic to compress. Keep as background only.
- **ATAA (audiovisual translators), SFT publications**. Important professional bodies but oriented toward subtitling/literary translation; rules transfer poorly to UI strings and developer docs.
- **Académie française** as an operational source. Its dictionary and position papers carry prestige but not concrete software-writing rules; FranceTerme is the operational arm.
- **PromptHub / "awesome-prompts" French entries**. Predominantly anonymous, generic copywriting/SEO/marketing prompts; no editorial signal; excluded by brief.
- **GitLab's documentation style guide** as a French source. Excellent for English docs-as-code methodology, but the "translation" page is workflow/glossary only — there is no public French editorial rule set.
- **Apple Style Guide French** as a primary public source. The general Apple Style Guide is publicly viewable but is English-only; French localization guidance is mostly internal/vendor-NDAed and was not surfaced as a public artefact in this pass.
- **Wikipédia FR Conventions typographiques** as the primary typography source. Useful as a free, web-readable summary, but for any production skill the Imprimerie nationale *Lexique* and (for Canada) BDL/*Clés de la rédaction* should be authoritative; Wikipédia keep as a secondary cross-check.

## 5. Gaps and questions for Phase 2

- **Worked examples coverage.** Does each candidate actually carry *before/after* French examples (not just abstract rules)? Microsoft and WordPress.com clearly do; Mozilla varies; DSFR has UI examples but few prose ones; *Clés de la rédaction* has many.
- **Tone defaults.** Confirm per-source whether the default is vouvoiement, tutoiement, or impersonal/infinitive. WordPress.com mandates vous; Mozilla differs by product (Firefox Desktop formal, Firefox for Android and web content informal); OQLF documents tutoiement contexts; FranceTerme/Imprimerie nationale are silent (general prose).
- **fr-FR vs fr-CA divergence cataloguing.** Build a small explicit diff list (courriel/email, témoin/cookie, baladodiffusion/podcast, fichier joint/pièce jointe, clavarder/chatter, ouvrir une session/se connecter, etc.) — the brief explicitly asks to surface this where it differs materially.
- **Anglicism rule overlap.** Microsoft, OQLF, FranceTerme, *Clés de la rédaction* and LanguageTool's `CAT_ANGLICISMES` all maintain anglicism lists; assess overlap and pick a curated subset of ~50–100 high-impact entries.
- **Typography rules: skill vs. linter.** Typography (NBSP before `?!:;` and inside `«…»`, "1re" not "1ère", capitales accentuées, no comma before a citation that ends with "…") is mechanical and belongs in a *linter* (Grammalecte/LanguageTool/Vale) rather than the LLM prompt. Verify in Phase 2 that these can be enforced post-generation.
- **UI-string-specific edge cases.** ICU plural categories, gendered placeholders ("{username} a {action,select,...}"), and constrained UI lengths (Microsoft says no `(s)`-style flexion). Confirm that the skill encodes ICU syntax preservation and forbids "le(s)" patterns.
- **Inclusive writing policy conflict.** WordPress.com says "rédiger épicène lorsque c'est possible"; the Canadian *Écriture inclusive – Lignes directrices* from the Bureau de la traduction codifies inclusive practice; Microsoft fr-FR mandates the opposite — "When addressing users, always use the masculine gender" (§3.1.8, June 2017). Phase 2 must surface this conflict and recommend a configurable rule (default: épicène where natural, masculine fallback for terseness, with a fr-CA mode that leans inclusive).
- **Skill compressibility.** Microsoft's 46-page PDF and DSFR's hundreds of pages cannot be ingested wholesale. Phase 2 must test whether the *operational* rules compress to ~500–2000 lines, leaving terminology and typography in external lookup tables.
- **Maintenance of OSS sources.** The Mozilla guide is alive; the Sun *Guide stylistique français* used by GNOME-fr is excellent but stale (2000s); KDE-fr Dico is alive but svn-driven. Phase 2 must weight maintenance.
- **Sources for AI-tics specifically.** Beyond `alxbd/boileau` and the StealthyLabsHQ "plume-naturelle" catalogue, are there serious editor blog posts or French translator threads documenting LLM tells in French? This area has the thinnest evidence; consider crawling Hacker News / Mastodon francophone / SFT and ATAA member blogs in Phase 2 (strictly as failure-mode catalogues, never as detector-evasion inputs).
- **Where does each rule live?** Tentative partition for Phase 2 to validate: (a) **LLM skill** — tone, register, vous/tu, anglicism replacements, AI-tic phrases, paragraph rhythm, infinitif-vs-impératif for UI; (b) **Linter (Vale + LanguageTool + Grammalecte)** — typography, NBSP, capitalisation, accents, repetition; (c) **Glossary / translation memory** — terminology (FranceTerme + GDT + project-specific); (d) **Human checklist** — judgement calls on inclusive writing, brand voice, edge-case product names.
